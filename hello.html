INTRODUCTION

What are Volcanoes?

Definition

An opening on a planet's or moon's surface known as a volcano allows material from the interior of the object, which is warmer than its surrounds, to escape. It produces an eruption when this material exits. Unusual amounts of material may be launched into the sky during an eruption. Alternately, it might be more serene with soft material flows.














What is Volcanic Eruption?

When lava and gas are released from a volcano, often explosively, this is known as an eruption. A "glowing avalanche," which occurs when recently erupted lava slides down the flanks of a volcano, is the most hazardous type of eruption.















How does an eruption occur?

When material from a planet's or moon's interior erupts onto the surface of the object from its inside, a volcano is created. On Earth, the erupted material can be gas, cinders, ash, or liquid rock (referred to as "lava" when it is on the surface and "magma" when it is underground). There are three potential causes for magma to ascend and explode onto the surface of the Earth. Because of how hot it is deep below the Earth, some rocks slowly melt and turn into lava, a viscous fluid. Since magma is lighter than the surrounding solid rock, it rises and gathers in magma chambers. Some of the magma eventually surges through fissures and vents to the Earth's surface.











What is Hazard?

A source or circumstance that has the potential to cause harm to people or the environment, damage to property, or any combination of these is referred to as a hazard.












What is Volcanic Eruption Hazard?

When lava and gas are released from a volcano, often explosively, this is known as an eruption. A "glowing avalanche," which occurs when recently erupted lava slides down the flanks of a volcano, is the most hazardous type of eruption. They are capable of swift movement and can reach temperatures of 1,200 degrees Fahrenheit. Other dangers include lahars and ash fall (mud or debris flows). Food shortages and population dislocation are frequent effects of volcanoes.














WHAT TO DO

Before, During, and After

BEFORE

Things to do BEFORE a volcanic eruption:

1. Prepare your go bag.


Your go bag should contain flashlight and extra batteries, first aid kit and manual, emergency food and water, essential medicines, respiratory (breathing) protection, eye protection (goggles), and battery-powered radio.


2. Know the evacuation routes.


Avoid the volcano's downwind and downstream river valleys. The wind and gravity will carry debris and ash. If you have enough supplies, take temporary refuge from volcanic ash where you are.


3. Charge your devices and gas up your car.


When a volcanic eruption occurs, fuel up your car and charge your electronics to be ready. Communication during natural disasters may be very helpful, as can mobility if you need to get to a safe spot quickly.


4. Stay indoors.


Stay inside since volcanic ash poses a health risk, especially if you suffer from a respiratory condition like asthma or bronchitis. Close all windows and doors when inside to prevent volcanic ash from entering. Place wet towels at doorways. Avoid clogging up phone lines with unnecessary calls.


5. Be updated to local news and alert warnings.


Keep up with local news on volcanic eruptions to determine whether you should evacuate or what to do when one occurs. Pay attention to alert warnings if they are issued because they may serve as a guide for you during eruptions.

DURING

Things to do DURING a volcanic eruption:

1. Evacuate or shelter somewhere safe.


In times of volcanic eruption, it is a must that we are in a safer place rather than going anywhere.


2. Too much ash fall: do NOT drive.


When there is too much ash falling, avoid driving to keep us safe on the road.


3. Wearing masks and goggles are needed.


As the ash fall is happening when a volcanic eruption occurs, wearing a mask and goggles are needed so that we are prone to having serious sickness.


4. Assist children, pregnant women, & elders.


The children, pregnant women, elders– as well as PWDs are the ones who are the priorities in case of volcanic eruptions.


5. Stay away from rivers and streams


As much as possible, we should avoid going to rivers and streams as volcanoes can blast lava and other elements coming out from the volcano.

AFTER

Things to do AFTER a volcanic eruption

1. Check yourself and your family.


After the earthquake, check yourself and your family for any injuries or bruises. Make sure to be ready for possible aftershocks.


2. Stay indoors until further announcements.


Stay indoors and wait for the announcement of the local government to ensure the safety of the people, animals, and properties.


3. Check the damage it caused to your house.


Damage is prone to occur during volcanic eruptions. After ensuring the safety of you and your family, check the well-being of your house in every corner.


4. Wear a mask when cleaning.


Volcanic eruptions produce ash. The most common and widespread volcanic hazard is ash fall. Cleaning the ash left by the phenomenon is essential.


5. Remove ashes from your roof to prevent collapse.


Ash falls’ weight may differ and vary, its chemical form may a ffect one’s building structure. To prevent further damage, remove ashes immediately as soon as possible.
